# Learning-to-Learn: Evidence → Practice

The operating principle is simple: **make memory produce the answer, then bring the
same dependency back after time has passed.** Reading, highlighting, and immediate
fluency can support study, but they are weak ownership tests.

## What the evidence supports

| Finding | Confidence | Operational consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Practice testing and distributed practice have broad support across materials and settings. Dunlosky et al. rated both **high utility**. | High | Replace “review the note” with “produce or correct from memory,” then revisit later. |
| Combining retrieval with spacing is a practical core rather than two separate habits. Carpenter, Pan, and Butler review evidence across ages, domains, and applied settings. | High | Every important error or theorem gets both a retrieval task and a future due date. |
| Interleaving can improve discrimination between similar problem types, but Dunlosky et al. rated the 2013 evidence **moderate**, with boundary conditions. | Moderate | Interleave concepts that are easily confused; do not randomize everything indiscriminately. |
| In Kapur's mathematics studies, problem solving before instruction improved conceptual understanding and transfer relative to instruction first, while both routes supported procedural knowledge. | Moderate and scoped | Attempt a structurally meaningful problem before reading the canonical solution. Do not confuse unguided struggle with a complete curriculum. |

The 1-week → 1-month → 3-month schedule below is an **operational heuristic**, not a
claim that these papers identify one universally optimal interval. Adjust it to the
retention horizon and performance.

## System A — IELTS writing vault

### Replace correction storage with correction retrieval

For each recurring error:

1. Save the error tag and the smallest diagnostic context.
2. On review, hide the correction.
3. Require a fresh sentence or micro-task that exercises the same dependency.
4. Require the correction **and one short reason**. Recognizing the right answer or
   naming the grammar rule does not pass.
5. Give corrective feedback immediately after the attempt.

### Schedule it

- Initial successful correction → re-probe in about 1 week.
- Pass → re-probe in about 1 month.
- Pass again → re-probe in about 3 months.
- Fail or repeat the error in a new essay → mark `needs-work` and keep it visible.

Rotate cues: new topic → boundary case → one-feature perturbation → explain why.
Never reuse the original sentence verbatim. A progress report surfaces the
oldest-due error before letting recent essays dominate attention.

### Success criterion

The learner produces the correct form in a fresh context and explains the relevant
constraint without opening the old feedback.

## System B — Measure-theory roadmap

### Productive-failure theorem loop

For each theorem:

1. Read only the statement, definitions, and prerequisites.
2. Spend a bounded 15–25 minutes trying to prove it, disprove it, or identify the
   missing bridge.
3. Write the attempted proof spine and the exact point of failure.
4. Read the proof and compare strategies: which assumption or construction supplied
   the missing bridge?
5. Close the source and reconstruct the proof spine from memory.

This is an extrapolation from productive-failure research in mathematics education,
not a directly tested measure-theory protocol. The time box prevents “struggle
first” from becoming unbounded floundering.

### Space and interleave

Re-probe the theorem after roughly 1 week, 1 month, and 3 months. Change the cue:
derive a key line, weaken a hypothesis, construct a boundary example, or explain why
one step is load-bearing.

Interleave the modes of convergence after each has been introduced:

- given a sequence, decide which mode is plausible before calculating;
- compare almost-sure, in-probability, $L^p$, and distributional convergence on
  neighboring examples;
- ask which implication needs an extra hypothesis and construct the smallest
  counterexample when it fails.

Blocked practice is useful during first acquisition. Interleaving becomes useful
when the target is choosing among similar tools rather than merely executing one
named procedure.

### Success criterion

The learner selects the relevant convergence notion, reconstructs the theorem's
proof spine, and predicts the first failure under a weakened hypothesis—without
seeing the theorem label or proof.

## Weekly system check

- Did every load-bearing item require production rather than recognition?
- Does every passed item have a future due date?
- Did at least one old error or theorem return this week?
- Was the cue materially different from the previous probe?
- Did a failed attempt produce feedback and a concrete repair action?

## Sources and scope

- John Dunlosky et al., “Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning
  Techniques” (2013), *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*.
  [Publisher record and full article](https://doi.org/10.1177/1529100612453266)
- Shana K. Carpenter, Steven C. Pan, and Andrew C. Butler, “The Science of
  Effective Learning with Spacing and Retrieval Practice” (2022), *Nature Reviews
  Psychology*. [Publisher article](https://doi.org/10.1038/s44159-022-00089-1)
- Manu Kapur, “Productive Failure in Learning Math” (2014), *Cognitive Science*.
  [Publisher article](https://doi.org/10.1111/cogs.12107)

The evidence is strongest for the general strategy families. The specific vault
schemas, interval sequence, cue rotation, theorem time box, and measure-theory
applications are design decisions derived from those findings and should be revised
against actual learner performance.
