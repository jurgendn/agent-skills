# Proposal & research-plan craft

Craft guidance for the *forward-looking* parts of an application — the research plan,
the fellowship proposal, the "what I want to do next" section of an SOP. This is where
an SOP stops summarizing the past and has to convince a committee to bet on a future.

Source: Knapen, Boffin, Chamba, Chamba, *How to write competitive proposals and job
applications*, Nature Astronomy 9, 951 (2025), arXiv:2504.01645. Domain examples are
astronomy, but the writing principles transfer directly to research-plan and
proposal-style sections of PhD/fellowship applications.

## Research statement vs. research plan — know which you're writing

A recurring, costly confusion. A **research statement** is mostly a summary of *past*
research. A **research plan** is a *proposal* for research to be undertaken if
admitted/hired — and it follows proposal rules (below). Many SOP prompts blend both;
identify which the prompt actually asks for, and don't answer a "plan" prompt with a
retrospective, or vice versa.

## The hourglass structure

Because the reader is often *not* an expert in your niche:
- **Open wide**: why this research area matters and what the problem is — with an
  emphasis on why it needs attention *now*.
- **Funnel in**: the specific questions you'll pursue and how you'll attack them.
- **Open back up**: how the field moves forward once the work is done.

Avoid generic openers ("The study of X is one of the key fields of modern science")
— they tell the reader nothing. Open with a sentence that raises a concrete,
interesting question and grabs attention.

## The four highest-leverage parts

Panels and triage readers often decide on these before reading everything else, so
spend disproportionate effort here:
- **Title / headline framing** — catchy, relevant, keyword-rich; drop filler words
  ("unveiling", vague qualifiers).
- **Abstract / opening paragraph** — the whole excitement in one paragraph, jargon-
  free, hourglass-shaped. Expect to rewrite it many times.
- **Opening paragraph of the body** — the key to explaining the problem; where you
  hook the specific reader.
- **Conclusion** — where you restate what success unlocks.

## Word-level discipline

- **Make every sentence count.** Cut dead wood: "the majority of" → "most".
- **Kill claims you can't back:** avoid "unique" (can you prove it?), "maximise" (be
  quantitative instead), "I believe"/"I think" (state facts, not faith).
- **Keep sentences short and clear.** Your native language may push you toward longer
  sentences; resist — reviewers are busy and reward readability. No contractions, no
  exclamation points, limit jargon and abbreviations.
- **Project enthusiasm, don't boast.** "Remarkable" is fine; there's a fine line, and
  peer feedback is how you find it.
- **Avoid negative phrasing.** Negativity bias makes negative statements stick harder
  and read as harder to parse; if you must state a caveat, don't put it in the first
  or last sentence of a paragraph.
- **Beware bias in self-presentation.** How positively research is framed, and how it
  lands, is influenced by gender and culture. This is a known asymmetry, not a reason
  to undersell; get peer feedback on tone.

## Process

- Study the prompt/call and its evaluation criteria *first*; write to the criteria,
  not to what's easiest for you.
- Summarize the core idea in **one sentence**, then **one paragraph**, then derive the
  goals from it and build outward.
- Outline → quick complete first draft → spend the remaining time revising. Play
  "critical reviewer" of your own draft. Set your *own* deadline ahead of the real one
  to leave room for review by others, including non-experts in your subfield.

## On AI assistance (for the applicant, and for you helping them)

Use LLMs to polish *your own* draft's grammar/clarity and to get unstuck on a title or
sentence — not to write from scratch. LLMs predict from historical language, whereas a
competitive proposal must present *new* ideas; they are verbose, can invent facts and
references, and homogenize voice across sections (which reviewers notice). Always treat
LLM output as a draft to edit, correct, and fact-check, and watch for words you would
not normally use. Note also that many funders forbid pasting proposals into external
tools — check before using any.

## Resubmission (applications, unlike papers, get reused)

Most proposals and applications fail the first time; the same proposal, brutally
rejected once, is often accepted on resubmission. When iterating:
- Never take rejection personally — it's of the document at a moment, not of you.
- Read the feedback critically (ask for it if not provided) and act on it.
- Make it clearer, shorter, more compelling; remove vague/ambiguous statements and
  errors.
- Strengthen the case with new evidence (published progress, pilot results).
- Keep a "CV of failures" perspective: every scientist has more rejections than wins.
