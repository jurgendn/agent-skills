# Serving on a program committee (PC member / area chair)

Writing one review is a subset of PC work. A PC member (in TCS terms; ≈ "area chair"
in ML/NLP venues) is *responsible* for a pile of submissions: bidding on them, deciding
which to review personally vs delegate, managing subreviewers, driving discussion, and
helping reach decisions. This reference covers that process; the rest of the skill
covers writing the review itself.

Source: Dulek, Jeffery, Majenz, Schaffner, Speelman, de Wolf, *A Guide for New Program
Committee Members at Theoretical Computer Science Conferences* (2021), arXiv:2105.02773.
Norms are TCS/CS-conference-flavored; the mechanics generalize to any PC/AC role.

## Timeline

Invitation → call for papers → **bidding** → **review** → review deadline →
**discussion** (+ rebuttal) → decision → notification → wrap-up (invited speakers,
awards, session chairing). Workload spikes right after the submission deadline and
again during discussion.

## Before accepting

Only accept for venues you know and would submit to; get subreviewing experience first
so you know the time cost. Budget **2–3 hours per paper minimum** (more as a junior
member), excluding discussion. Be wary of spam/predatory invitations — you should never
pay to be on a PC.

## Bidding phase

- Skim all submissions (titles/abstracts; open PDFs when unsure). Bid to express
  interest, and — just as importantly — bid *negatively* on papers you don't want.
- **Use the full range** of bid points, not just the extremes; the assignment optimizer
  works better with spread.
- You can confidently bid on (and later reject) papers clearly out of scope even
  outside your expertise.
- **Declare conflicts of interest.** Standard hard COIs: your own papers, same-institute
  authors, your (former) students/advisors/recent postdocs, family/close relationships,
  or any paper where you can't be impartial ("previous history"). A hard-COI paper
  disappears from your view entirely; a "soft COI" (competing paper, old loose
  connection) you disclose in comments to the PC. When in doubt, ask the chair.
- Ethics: at anonymous venues, don't google the title to deanonymize. For submissions
  close to your own work, either declare COI or review but flag the potential COI to the
  PC — a *negative* review of a competitor needs solid technical justification.

## Review phase — as a coordinator

- Decide which of your pile to review yourself vs. delegate. PC members typically
  self-review about half. Keep for yourself: papers you'd read anyway, papers near your
  expertise, and easy dismissals (out of scope, clearly flawed).
- **Contact subreviewers within a day** — the obvious expert is being asked by others
  too. Send the review guidelines and COI policy. Give them a deadline **2–3 days before
  yours** as a buffer, and keep a back-up list.
- Raise subreviewer response rates: ask early, personalize the request, ask people you
  know, and follow up (~3 days) since automated emails get spam-filtered. Ask for
  explicit confirmation; if none after a reminder, move on. Reserve time to review one
  or two yourself if a subreviewer vanishes.
- You remain responsible for every review in your pile. When a subreview comes in: read
  at least the paper's intro yourself (read fully if all reviewers are external and
  positive), edit/error-correct the text (this also protects the subreviewer's
  anonymity), and **recalibrate the score** to be consistent across your batch — note
  any changes in comments to the PC.
- Notify the chair of fixable bugs in otherwise-good papers; the authors may get a
  chance to rectify.

## Discussion phase

- You see others' reviews only after submitting yours — put your papers (and interesting
  others) on your watchlist. Start the discussion promptly where you disagree; revise
  your score if a co-reviewer surfaces information you missed (say, an unfixable proof
  error).
- **Focus on borderline cases** — clear accepts/rejects sort themselves out. Be
  proactive across the whole pool, not just your assigned pile: look for papers with
  unresolved reviewer conflict, misclassified papers, and good work in unfashionable
  areas that risks falling through the cracks. Don't assume someone else will catch it.
- In the final push, ~40 papers may compete for ~20 slots; a strong, well-argued voice
  has real influence when everyone else is tired. Decisions may end in an approval vote
  or (rarely) a physical meeting, whose outcomes tend to be even noisier.
- **Rebuttal:** read whether it actually addresses your concerns; forward to your
  subreviewer for input when useful.
- **Polishing:** reflect the PC discussion back to authors as a courtesy — especially
  the real reason for a reject — and tone down over-enthusiastic reviews on rejected
  papers so authors aren't left confused by conflicting signals.

## Submissions by PC members (including yours)

Often assigned more reviewers (e.g. five vs three) and held to a stricter bar; there's
usually a cap (~two) on how many a PC member may submit.

## Wrap-up

You may help pick invited speakers and best-(student)-paper awards (diversity input
welcomed), and chair a session: be on time, learn to pronounce author names, keep time,
and have a question ready if the audience has none.
