# IELTS Writing Task 2 Coach

Task 2 is a 40-minute, minimum-250-word academic essay. The candidate must respond to a prompt with a clear position, well-developed arguments, and supporting details. Task 2 carries roughly twice the weight of Task 1 in the final Writing band score.

## Use this when

- The user shares a Task 2 prompt and wants a draft or critique.
- The user wants a band estimate with actionable feedback.
- The user asks how to structure any Task 2 essay type.
- The user wants help with argument development, thesis statements, or conclusion writing.
- The user asks about Task 2 vocabulary, avoiding repetition, or paraphrasing the prompt.

## Do not use this when

- The user is working on Task 1 (data description). Use `ielts-writing-task1`.
- The user is asking about Speaking. Use `ielts-speaking-coach`.

## Band Descriptors (Task 2)

Scores are averaged equally across four criteria. Task 2 is weighted more heavily than Task 1 in the overall Writing band.

### Task Response (TR)

| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 9 | Fully addresses all parts of task; fully developed position with relevant, fully extended, and well-supported ideas |
| 8 | Addresses all parts; well-developed response with relevant, extended, and supported ideas |
| 7 | Addresses all parts; clear position throughout; extends and supports main ideas but may over-generalise or rely on personal examples rather than more objective substantiation |
| 6 | Addresses all parts though some may be more fully covered; relevant position but conclusions may become unclear or repetitive; relevant main ideas but some inadequately developed |
| 5 | Addresses task only partially; expresses a position but development not always clear; some main ideas present but lacking focus; ideas irrelevant, repetitive, or insufficiently supported |
| 4 | Minimal response or inappropriate format; position unclear; main ideas difficult to identify |
| 3 | Does not address task; very little relevance to task or inappropriate format |

### Coherence and Cohesion (CC)

| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 9 | Cohesion attracts no attention; paragraphing managed skilfully |
| 8 | Sequences information and ideas logically; manages all aspects of cohesion well; paragraphing sufficient and appropriate |
| 7 | Logically organises information and ideas; good overall progression; uses cohesive devices including reference and substitution appropriately, though may occasionally over/under-use; clear central topic within each paragraph |
| 6 | Arranges information and ideas coherently; adequate overall progression; uses cohesive devices effectively but cohesion within/between sentences may be faulty or mechanical; referencing may be unclear; may lack overall progression |
| 5 | Presents information with some organisation but lacks overall progression; limited range of cohesive devices, not always accurately used; repetitive due to lack of referencing or substitution |
| 4 | Presents information and ideas but no clear progression; some basic cohesive devices, possibly inaccurate or repetitive; may not write in paragraphs or paragraphing may be inadequate |
| 3 | Does not organise ideas logically; very limited range of cohesive devices |

### Lexical Resource (LR)

| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 9 | Wide vocabulary with very natural and sophisticated control; rare minor errors only as slips |
| 8 | Wide vocabulary fluently and flexibly used to convey precise meanings; skilful use of uncommon items; rare errors in spelling or word formation |
| 7 | Sufficient range for flexibility and precision; less common items used with some awareness of style and collocation; occasional errors in word choice, spelling, or word formation |
| 6 | Adequate range for task; attempts less common vocabulary with some inaccuracy; some spelling or word formation errors but communication not impeded |
| 5 | Limited but minimally adequate vocabulary; noticeable errors in spelling or word formation that may cause some difficulty |
| 4 | Basic vocabulary only, possibly repetitive or inappropriate; limited word formation and spelling control; errors may cause strain |
| 3 | Very limited words and expressions; severe errors may distort the message |

### Grammatical Range and Accuracy (GRA)

| Band | Descriptor |
|------|-----------|
| 9 | Wide range of structures with full flexibility and accuracy; rare slips only |
| 8 | Wide range of structures; majority of sentences error-free; only very occasional errors or inappropriacies |
| 7 | Variety of complex structures; frequent error-free sentences; good grammar and punctuation control with few errors |
| 6 | Mix of simple and complex forms; some grammar and punctuation errors but rarely impeding communication |
| 5 | Limited range; complex sentence attempts tend to be less accurate than simple ones; frequent grammatical errors; faulty punctuation |
| 4 | Very limited structures; rare subordinate clauses; errors predominate; punctuation often faulty |
| 3 | Sentence forms attempted but errors and punctuation problems predominate and distort meaning |

## Essay Types and Structures

### 1. Opinion / Agree–Disagree
Prompt signals: *"To what extent do you agree or disagree?"*, *"Do you think…?"*, *"Give your opinion."*

Structure:
1. **Introduction** — Paraphrase prompt + clear thesis (your position)
2. **Body 1** — First reason supporting your view + example/explanation
3. **Body 2** — Second reason supporting your view + example/explanation
4. **Conclusion** — Restate position + final thought (no new information)

Avoid sitting on the fence unless you have a genuine nuanced position — vague "it depends" responses score lower on TR.

### 2. Discuss Both Views
Prompt signals: *"Discuss both views and give your opinion."*

Structure:
1. **Introduction** — Paraphrase prompt + state you will discuss both sides + hint at your view
2. **Body 1** — First view + supporting points
3. **Body 2** — Second view + supporting points
4. **Conclusion** — Your opinion + synthesis

The opinion must be explicit; omitting it drops TR.

### 3. Advantages and Disadvantages
Prompt signals: *"Discuss the advantages and disadvantages."*, *"Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?"*

Structure:
1. **Introduction** — Paraphrase + state you will examine pros and cons (+ your view if asked)
2. **Body 1** — Advantages + development
3. **Body 2** — Disadvantages + development
4. **Conclusion** — Overall judgement

### 4. Problem–Solution
Prompt signals: *"What are the causes…? What solutions can be suggested?"*, *"What problems does this cause and how can they be solved?"*

Structure:
1. **Introduction** — Paraphrase + state you will examine problems and solutions
2. **Body 1** — Problems (2–3 well-developed)
3. **Body 2** — Solutions (matched to problems)
4. **Conclusion** — Summary

### 5. Two-Part Question
Prompt signals: *"Why is this the case? What can be done about it?"*

Structure:
1. **Introduction** — Paraphrase + state you will address both questions
2. **Body 1** — Answer question 1 fully
3. **Body 2** — Answer question 2 fully
4. **Conclusion** — Summary

Both questions must be answered or TR is penalised.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Identify essay type and all parts of the task

Read the prompt carefully:
- How many questions does it ask? (Missing one = automatic TR penalty)
- What type is it? (Affects structure)
- What position or balance does the prompt expect?

### Step 2 — Plan before writing

Spend 5 minutes planning:
- Thesis/position
- 2 body paragraph topics
- 1–2 supporting points per paragraph with a specific example or explanation

A good plan prevents off-topic essays.

### Step 3 — Draft or critique

When drafting:
- Introduction: paraphrase + thesis. Never copy the prompt verbatim.
- Each body paragraph: topic sentence → explanation → example → link back.
- Stay at ≥250 words. Under-length essays are penalised on TR.
- Avoid bullet points — examiners expect continuous prose.

When critiquing a user's essay:
- First check TR: does the essay answer all parts? Is the position clear and consistent?
- Then check CC: is the progression logical? Are discourse markers appropriate (not mechanical)?
- Then LR and GRA.

### Step 4 — Score and feedback

Report a band estimate for each criterion and an estimated overall band:

```
Task Response:        X / 9
Coherence & Cohesion: X / 9
Lexical Resource:     X / 9
Grammatical Range:    X / 9
Estimated overall:    X.X / 9
```

For each criterion below 7, give 2–3 specific, actionable improvements with examples drawn from the user's actual text.

## Key language banks

### Discourse markers for essays

- **Adding**: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally
- **Contrasting**: However, Nevertheless, On the other hand, Conversely, Despite this
- **Cause/effect**: As a result, Consequently, Therefore, This leads to
- **Exemplifying**: For instance, For example, To illustrate, A case in point is
- **Conceding**: Although, While it is true that, Admittedly
- **Concluding**: In conclusion, To conclude, Overall, In summary

### Paraphrasing the prompt

Replace key words with synonyms or restructure the sentence:
- "increasingly common" → "becoming more prevalent / widespread"
- "young people" → "the younger generation / adolescents"
- "government" → "authorities / policymakers"
- "important" → "crucial / significant / vital"

### Expressing opinion

- It is argued that / It can be contended that (distancing)
- I firmly believe that / I strongly agree that (direct)
- While X has merit, Y is more convincing because…
- The evidence suggests that…

## Common mistakes to flag

1. **Answering only part of the task** — re-read the prompt for multiple questions; address all.
2. **Unclear or absent thesis** — the examiner must know your position by the end of the introduction.
3. **Mechanical discourse markers** — "Firstly… Secondly… Thirdly…" used without real development scores low on CC.
4. **Unsupported generalisations** — every claim needs either an example, a reason, or a concession.
5. **Under 250 words** — explicit penalty to TR.
6. **Bullet points or numbered lists** — write in paragraphs.
7. **Changing position mid-essay** — the position should be consistent throughout.
8. **Copying the prompt** — introduction must paraphrase, not copy.

## Output format

When producing a model essay:
- Label each paragraph (Introduction / Body 1 / Body 2 / Conclusion).
- Provide a word count.
- After the essay, give a brief note on structure and language choices.

When giving feedback on the user's essay:
- Quote the user's original text when identifying issues.
- Provide a corrected or improved version of the problem sentence.
- End with the scored table and top 3 priorities for improvement.
