# Error Taxonomy + Vietnamese-L1 Transfer Notes

The code set the coach uses to tag errors. Keep codes stable — the error ledger
keys on them, and consistent codes are what let recurring errors be tracked across
sessions. Assign the **most specific** code that fits; if two apply, log both.

The **L1** rows explain *why* a Vietnamese speaker tends to make the error.
Vietnamese is an analytic, uninflected language: nouns don't mark number, verbs
don't conjugate, time is shown by adverbs/particles (đã, đang, sẽ) rather than
morphology, and there are no articles. Most high-frequency errors below are direct
consequences. Naming the contrast is the coaching move that turns a fix into a
habit — but only state an L1 note when it is actually accurate for that error.

---

## The codes

### ART — Articles (a / an / the / zero)
Wrong or missing article; using "the" for a general class; "a" before a plural or
uncountable noun.
- Ex: *He is doctor.* → *He is **a** doctor.*  *I like the music.* (general) → *I like music.*
- **L1:** Vietnamese has no articles at all. This is the single most frequent
  transfer error — articles are dropped or inserted by guess. Heuristic: singular
  countable noun ⇒ it needs *some* determiner (a/an/the/my/this…).

### NUM — Number / countability
Missing plural `-s`; pluralizing an uncountable noun; wrong count/non-count treatment.
- Ex: *I have three book.* → *three book**s***.  *many informations* → *much information*.
- **L1:** Vietnamese marks plurality lexically (with classifiers/quantifiers), not on
  the noun, so plural `-s` is systematically dropped — especially after a number,
  where it feels redundant. Heuristic: a number/"many"/"several" before a countable
  noun ⇒ `-s`, every time.

### SVA — Subject–verb agreement
Verb doesn't agree with subject in person/number.
- Ex: *He go to work.* → *He **goes***.  *The results shows…* → *The results **show***.
- **L1:** Vietnamese verbs never change for the subject, so 3rd-person singular `-s`
  has no L1 anchor and is dropped under pressure. Heuristic: he/she/it + present
  verb ⇒ `-s`.

### TENSE — Tense choice
Wrong tense for the time frame (past event in present, etc.).
- Ex: *Yesterday I go there.* → *Yesterday I **went** there.*
- **L1:** Vietnamese shows time with words (hôm qua = yesterday, đã = already), so the
  verb itself stays bare and English past `-ed` gets dropped once the time-word is
  present. Heuristic: a past time-marker still requires the past-tense verb form.

### ASP — Aspect (simple / progressive / perfect)
Right time frame, wrong aspect: perfect vs. simple past, progressive misuse.
- Ex: *I live here since 2020.* → *I **have lived** here since 2020.*  *I am knowing…* → *I **know**…*
- **L1:** đang≈progressive and đã≈completed don't map cleanly onto English aspect;
  "since/for + present perfect" and the ban on progressive with stative verbs are
  both absent in Vietnamese.

### VF — Verb form (non-finite, gerund/infinitive, participle)
Wrong non-finite form; gerund vs. infinitive after a verb/preposition; bare vs. `to`.
- Ex: *I enjoy to read.* → *I enjoy **reading***.  *I look forward to see you.* → *to **seeing***.
- **L1:** Vietnamese has no infinitive/gerund distinction (verbs don't inflect), so
  which form follows a given verb/preposition must be learned item by item.

### MOD — Modals & conditionals
Modal + wrong verb form; conditional structure errors.
- Ex: *He can goes.* → *He can **go***.  *If I will have time…* → *If I **have** time…*
- **L1:** Modality is expressed by separate words in Vietnamese with no effect on the
  main verb, so the "modal + bare infinitive" and "no *will* in the if-clause" rules
  are unfamiliar.

### PREP — Prepositions
Wrong preposition; missing/extra preposition; verb+preposition collocation.
- Ex: *depend of* → *depend **on***.  *married with* → *married **to***.  *discuss about* → *discuss* (no prep).
- **L1:** Preposition mappings between Vietnamese and English don't align, so these are
  largely collocational and must be memorized, not derived. Common enough to often be
  systematic → ledger-worthy.

### WO — Word order
Adjective/adverb/question/clause order.
- Ex: *a car red* → *a **red car***.  *I know where is he.* → *…where **he is***.
- **L1:** Vietnamese puts modifiers *after* the noun (danh từ + tính từ), so
  "adjective before noun" and embedded-question order (no inversion) invert the L1 habit.

### WF — Word form (part of speech / derivation)
Right root, wrong part of speech.
- Ex: *It was a success experiment.* → *a **successful** experiment.*  *He acted very careless.* → *very **carelessly***.
- **L1:** Vietnamese uses the same word across parts of speech without suffixes, so
  English derivational endings (-ful, -ly, -tion, -ness) get omitted or misapplied.

### WC — Word choice / collocation
Grammatically possible but unnatural word or collocation (do/make, say/tell).
- Ex: *do a mistake* → ***make** a mistake.*  *say me the answer* → ***tell** me…*
- Not always a "grammar" error, but flag it — collocations are a common Band-7 ceiling.

### PRON — Pronoun & reference
Wrong/ambiguous pronoun; missing subject/object; unclear "it/this/they".
- Ex: *Is raining.* → ***It** is raining.*  *The team, they won.* (redundant) → *The team won.*
- **L1:** Vietnamese freely drops pronouns (pro-drop) and often repeats nouns, so
  English's obligatory subjects (dummy *it/there*) and its dislike of redundant
  resumptive pronouns both cause trouble.

### CONJ — Connectors & linking
Wrong/overused connector; comma splice from a connector; "although…but".
- Ex: *Although it was late, but we stayed.* → drop **but** (one connector per link).
- **L1:** Vietnamese uses paired connectives (tuy…nhưng = although…but), which
  transfers as the double-connector error in English.

### PUNC — Punctuation affecting grammar
Sentence fragment, run-on, comma splice — only when it breaks grammatical structure.
- Ex: *I was tired, I went home.* → semicolon / *so* / two sentences.
- Ignore purely stylistic punctuation; flag only structure-breaking cases.

## Using this with the ledger

When you log a systematic error, use its **code** as the key. If the same code recurs
across sessions it's a genuine gap — prioritize it for drilling and re-probes. The
Vietnamese-transfer codes (ART, NUM, SVA, TENSE, ASP, PREP, WO) are the ones most
likely to be systematic for this user; watch them first.
