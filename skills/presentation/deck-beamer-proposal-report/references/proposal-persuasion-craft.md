# Proposal persuasion craft (for the proposal deck)

The proposal spine in this skill covers *what goes on which slide*. This file covers
*how to make the proposal persuasive* — the rhetorical craft that decides whether a
committee/funder feels the plan is exciting and feasible, not just complete.

Source: Knapen, Boffin, Chamba, Chamba, *How to write competitive proposals and job
applications*, Nature Astronomy 9, 951 (2025), arXiv:2504.01645. Written for research
proposals; the framing and figure guidance map cleanly onto proposal-defense slides.

## Frame it as a sell, not an info dump

A proposal is a persuasion tool, not an information package: it must convince the panel
to approve *your* plan over the others competing for the same slot. A winning proposal
tends to combine: (1) relevance/excitement legible even to a non-expert reviewer;
(2) an outcome that reads as **fundamental, not incremental**; (3) a credible reason
*you* can do it (unique skill or idea); (4) a sound methodology and convincing
timeline; and (5) above all, a **story the reviewer wants to see the end of**. On
slides, make the through-line a narrative, not a list of work packages.

## The hourglass, on slides

Reviewers are rarely experts in your exact niche. Open wide (why the area matters, why
*now*), funnel to the specific problem and your approach, then open back up to what the
field gains once the work is done. The problem/significance and impact slides are the
wide ends; objectives/approach are the neck.

## The three figures that make a proposal (Kuchner)

A strong proposal deck earns its keep with figures, not text. Three archetypes, each
doing a distinct job — include the ones your slot has room for; in a short deck the
before/after usually earns its place first:

- **The "beautiful butterfly"** — an attention-grabbing opener that sets the stage (a
  striking image, a compelling visualization). Personalize it; avoid the same
  review-paper figure everyone in your field has shown for a decade.
- **The "family portrait"** — the state of the art in one figure, showing you know the
  landscape and where your work fits (e.g. all prior results on one axis, with the gap
  your proposal fills highlighted).
- **The "before/after"** — current state beside the state *if the proposal succeeds*.
  The more striking the difference, the stronger the pull. This is often the single
  most convincing slide in a small proposal.

Match figures to the panel: subject-expert panels don't need the pretty picture; a
mixed or cross-disciplinary panel may be won by it. Keep every figure legible and fully
explained; a fresh version of a published figure ("based on Author et al.") beats a
cluttered original.

## Density and the four leverage points

- **Don't cram.** Packing maximum text into the space frightens reviewers. Break up
  blocks with whitespace, figures, and highlight — without turning the slide into a
  "graphical Christmas tree."
- **Concentrate on one or two questions,** not many related issues.
- Disproportionate care goes to the **title, the opening, the key figures, and the
  summary/impact** — a triage reviewer may decide on these alone.

## Report decks: honesty is the persuasion

For progress/final reports, credibility comes from *not* hiding slippage. Show
at-risk/missed milestones with their mitigation; a status view that buries problems
destroys trust faster than the problems themselves. Keep budget, timeline, and
milestone numbers consistent across every slide — they will be checked.
