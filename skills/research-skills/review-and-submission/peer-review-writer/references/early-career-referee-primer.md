# Early-career referee primer

Use this when the user is new to reviewing, lacks confidence in their judgment,
or asks whether a criticism is fair, too harsh, or too weak.

Grounding: Ntampaka, Bonaca, Bose, Eisenstein, Hadzhiyska, Mason, Nagai,
Speagle, *A Referee Primer for Early Career Astronomers*, arXiv:2205.14270.
The paper is astronomy-focused, but the review stance transfers to conference
and journal reviewing in most research fields.

## Calibrate the role

A referee is not an adversary and not a proofreader. The job is to help the
editor/area chair decide whether the paper's claims are supported, important,
and clear enough for the venue, while giving the authors specific paths to
improve the work.

Early-career failure modes:
- **Over-lenient:** avoids hard calls because the reviewer lacks confidence.
- **Over-harsh:** treats severity as proof of expertise.
- **Template-bound:** fills the form but never states the decision-relevant
  argument.

Corrective stance: be firm about evidence and generous about people. Critique
claims, methods, and presentation; do not speculate about author competence or
intent.

## What to look for

Before writing, identify:
- the central claim and whether the evidence actually supports it;
- novelty relative to the relevant literature, not just the papers the authors cite;
- whether methods, data, and uncertainty estimates are sufficient to trust the result;
- whether limitations are acknowledged honestly;
- which weaknesses are fatal, fixable, or minor.

Do not let small presentation defects dominate a review if the scientific claim
is sound. Conversely, polished writing does not rescue an unsupported central
claim.

## Writing the report

Lead with a neutral summary showing you understood the paper. Then give a small
number of prioritized major points, each with:
- the specific location in the paper;
- why it matters for the paper's main claim;
- what evidence, analysis, or clarification would resolve it.

Keep minor comments separate and compact. A long list of small edits is useful
only after the major decision signal is clear.

## Fairness pass

Before finalizing, check:
- Would this review help the authors revise, even if rejected?
- Is every strong criticism tied to a specific claim or missing evidence?
- Are you applying venue standards rather than personal taste?
- Did you disclose low confidence or expertise gaps where relevant?
- Would you stand behind the wording if your name were visible?
