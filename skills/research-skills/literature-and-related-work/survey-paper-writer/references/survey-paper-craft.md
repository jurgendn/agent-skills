# Survey paper craft

Grounding:
- Niyato et al., *From Literature to Insights: Methodological Guidelines for
  Survey Writing in Communications Research*, arXiv:2509.25828.
- Baltes and Ralph, *Teaching Literature Reviewing for Software Engineering
  Research*, arXiv:2406.08369.
- Salatino et al., *A Survey on Knowledge Organization Systems of Research
  Fields*, arXiv:2409.04432.
- Li et al., *A Survey Forest Diagram*, arXiv:2407.17081.

## Topic selection

A survey topic is publishable when it is:
- important enough that readers need orientation;
- bounded enough to survey rigorously;
- changing enough that an updated synthesis is useful;
- confusing enough that a taxonomy or tutorial genuinely helps;
- not already covered by a recent, high-quality survey with the same angle.

The novelty is not "no one has surveyed X." Stronger novelty is a better
organization, a cross-community synthesis, a clearer tutorial path, or a sharper
future agenda.

## Literature collection

Use a transparent search strategy:
- seed with canonical papers and recent surveys;
- search by method names, problem names, benchmarks, datasets, and neighboring
  discipline vocabulary;
- follow citations backward for foundations and forward for recent uptake;
- include negative, limitation, and replication papers;
- record exclusion rules when the field is too large.

For broad fields, use a staged protocol: exploratory mapping first, then a more
systematic inclusion/exclusion pass after the taxonomy stabilizes.

## Taxonomy design

Prefer taxonomies that help decisions:
- Which method should I use under which assumptions?
- Which benchmark answers which question?
- Which failure mode does each line of work address?
- Which parts of the field are mature, unsettled, or overclaimed?

Avoid taxonomies that merely mirror venue categories or author communities unless
those categories explain technical differences.

## Writing sections

A strong survey section has:
1. a question or organizing distinction;
2. grouped representative papers;
3. a comparison of assumptions, evidence, and limitations;
4. a short synthesis of what the group teaches;
5. unresolved issues that motivate the next section or future work.

Do not write one paragraph per paper unless the section is explicitly historical.

## Figures and tables

Useful survey artifacts:
- taxonomy diagram;
- method comparison matrix;
- timeline of conceptual shifts;
- benchmark/dataset/resource table;
- pipeline diagram for tutorial papers;
- open-problems matrix.

Each artifact should reduce cognitive load. If a table has too many columns to
read, split by decision rather than shrinking the font.

## Future directions

Future directions should name:
- the unresolved question;
- the evidence gap;
- why current methods are insufficient;
- a concrete next experiment, theorem, dataset, benchmark, or system;
- the risk that could make the direction unproductive.

Prefer fewer, sharper directions over a long wishlist.
