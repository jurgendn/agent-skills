# Condensed source material for research-proposal writing

Durable distillations of the canonical guides, with links for live consultation.
Program-specific details (page limits, criteria wording, deadlines) are volatile —
always verify against the live official call rather than trusting a cached number.

## 1. The Heilmeier Catechism (DARPA)

<https://www.darpa.mil/about/heilmeier-catechism>

George Heilmeier's question set for evaluating proposed research; most funding
agencies' review criteria descend from it. The eight questions, near-verbatim:

1. What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.
2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
3. What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?
4. Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?
5. What are the risks?
6. How much will it cost?
7. How long will it take?
8. What are the mid-term and final "exams" to check for success?

Use: the skeleton in SKILL.md maps onto these; a proposal that cannot answer
one of them has a hole. Question 8 is the most commonly missing — checkable
milestones, not vibes.

## 2. Przeworski & Salomon, "The Art of Writing Proposals" (SSRC, 1988/1995)

<https://www.ssrc.org/publications/the-art-of-writing-proposals/>

The classic on committee psychology. Key points:

- Every reviewer scans for three answers: **what will we learn that we don't
  know now; why is it worth knowing; how will we know the conclusions are
  valid.** Reviewers work through tall stacks on donated time and won't dig.
- **The opening paragraph (first page at most) is the whole game.** Overstate
  the point there; add caveats later. A clearly posed non-rhetorical question or
  a bold central claim are both strong openings. Leave one memorable message —
  be "the one who claims X" in the committee discussion.
- **Methodology is an argument, not an agenda.** A list of tasks doesn't prove
  the tasks are the best attack on the problem. "I will look at the relation
  between x and y" names no research operation — specify the archives, sources,
  respondents, datasets, techniques of analysis, and the validity tests you
  commit to. *Most proposals fail because reviewers are left wondering what the
  applicant will actually do.*
- **Bibliography is a seriousness signal.** It shows the project complements
  rather than duplicates existing work; a single missing directly-relevant
  reference is costly.
- **Something must be at stake.** Convince the reader the inquiry is not
  tendentiously marching to a preconceived end; say what would prove you wrong.
- **Avoid the pack.** Hot topics are crowded because someone already made the
  decisive contribution; a genuinely fresh problem is worth more.
- **Explicit comparisons have special appeal** (cases chosen so similarities
  and differences illuminate the question — and you can execute both legs).
- **Name the final product** (article, dissertation chapter, venue class).
- Write for a **multidisciplinary committee**: jargon-free, ideas over
  apparatus, technical detail in appendices. Start months early; circulate
  drafts.

## 3. MIT Communication Lab CommKit — NSF GRFP Research Proposal

EECS: <https://mitcommlab.mit.edu/eecs/commkit/nsf-research-proposal/> ·
ChemE: <https://mitcommlab.mit.edu/cheme/commkit/nsf-fellowship-research-proposal/> ·
NSE: <https://mitcommlab.mit.edu/nse/commkit/nsf-research-proposal/>

Discipline-specific annotated guides. Key points for the PhD/fellowship mode:

- **Fellowships fund the person, not the project.** The proposal demonstrates
  you can pose feasible, original research — you are not bound to execute it.
  It's a critical-thinking exam, not a contract.
- Write for a **fatigued, interdisciplinary panel**: plain language, explicit
  "what and why."
- **Balance creativity with credibility** — neither trivial nor grandiose;
  achievable within a PhD timeline.
- **Name institutional resources** (equipment, advisors, collaborators at the
  target program) to show feasibility.
- **Concrete outcomes with clear endpoints** (an algorithm, a tool, a dataset).
- NSF mandates separate labeled **Intellectual Merit** and **Broader Impacts**
  sections; two-page limit. (Volatile — verify against the current solicitation.)

## 4. Simon Peyton Jones — research writing/proposal talks

<https://simon.peytonjones.org/great-research-paper/> (talks index; grant-proposal
talk on YouTube: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nEuK54bo6RE>)

- "It's an interesting area" is **not** a problem statement. State the crisp
  problem and your idea for attacking it in the first half page.
- The reader is tired and skimming; front-load the one idea you want them to
  keep.

## 5. PLOS Computational Biology "Ten Simple Rules" series

- Getting Grants: <https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.0020012>
- Writing a Postdoctoral Fellowship: <https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1004934>
- MSCA fellowship applications: <https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1010371>

Reviewer-side heuristics:

- **Align with the sponsor's mission** — fellowships judge whether the plan
  advances *their* goals (e.g., building independent investigators), not only
  the science.
- On resubmission, show explicitly that you understood prior criticism and
  precisely how each problem was fixed.
- EU proposals (MSCA, ERC-style) are structured around the three pillars
  **Excellence / Impact / Implementation** — the Implementation pillar is where
  work packages, milestones, risk tables, and Gantt-style timelines live.
  (Pillar wording and templates are volatile — verify against the live call.)
